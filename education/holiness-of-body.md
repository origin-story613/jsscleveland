title: The Holiness of the Body
subtitle: Why must we treat a body with respect, even after death?
---

Jewish law draws a distinction between two kinds of sacred objects. *Tashmishei Mitzvah* are items used to perform a mitzvah — a lulav, the s'chach of a sukkah, and so on. Once their use is complete, they may be discarded, because the holiness was in the act, not the object. *Tashmishei Kedusha*, by contrast, are items that contain the written Name of G-d or words of Torah — a Sefer Torah, tefillin, a mezuzah. These remain holy forever. Even if they are damaged beyond use, their sanctity never departs, and they must be buried rather than thrown away.

A human being, our tradition teaches, is like the second kind. Every Jew who spoke words of Torah, who said *Shema Yisrael*, becomes a vessel for holiness — much as parchment becomes sanctified by the words inscribed upon it. And so, even after the soul has departed, the holiness that was "inscribed" on a person during their lifetime remains. The body retains an eternal kedusha, and is treated with the same reverence we would give a Sefer Torah.

*Adapted from a teaching by Rabbi Yissocher Frand ([torah.org](https://torah.org)).*
