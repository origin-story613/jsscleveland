For Jews, burial in Israel is considered a highly virtuous practice — so much so that it is customary to sprinkle a little earth from Israel in the coffin of those buried elsewhere. This tradition is grounded in a number of Jewish sources, the best known of which may be the teaching that all Jews will ultimately be resurrected, and that, according to the Midrash, those buried in Israel will be resurrected first; those buried elsewhere will be brought to the Holy Land through underground tunnels. *(Content adapted from myjewishlearning.com.)*

Burial in Israel requires both the purchase of a burial plot and the logistical arrangements to prepare, transport, and inter the body. Arrangements must also be made to receive the body at the airport in Israel and transport it to the cemetery — typically handled by the burial society associated with the cemetery where the plot was purchased.

## Israel cemeteries and burial options

Plots can often be purchased directly from a cemetery, but it is usually easier to work through an intermediary. A number of American synagogues and Jewish organizations own blocks of cemetery plots that they resell to their members, and several organizations specialize in assisting Jews from abroad who wish to purchase plots in Israel.

## Where in Israel are burial plots available?

Jerusalem has always been the most sought-after location for burial in Israel, but space within the city is increasingly scarce and prices are substantial. The city has two major cemeteries — one in eastern Jerusalem, on the Mount of Olives overlooking the Old City, and the other, Har Hamenuchot, at the western entrance to the city. Lack of space at Har Hamenuchot has prompted the construction of extensive underground catacombs to meet demand.

Beit Shemesh, a city roughly 20 miles west of Jerusalem, has emerged as a popular alternative. Various Jewish organizations from around the world have reserved sections of the city's privately owned Eretz Hachaim cemetery, which also sells plots directly from its Brooklyn office. Plots are available in other Israeli cities as well, though space pressures have made some locations difficult to secure.
